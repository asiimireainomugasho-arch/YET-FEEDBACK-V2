Executive Summary

Six headline findings from 48 YET alumni ¬∑ February 2026 ¬∑ For stakeholder consideration

‚úÖUniversal Training Preparedness

100% of all 48 respondents ‚Äî across every cohort and location ‚Äî confirmed that YET training adequately prepared them for subsequent work. This is a near-unprecedented outcome for a programme of this scale and complexity, and deserves prominent placement in all donor and stakeholder communications.

üì°YET Alumni: The #1 Recruitment Channel

25 of 48 respondents (52%) first heard about YET through existing alumni. This organic peer advocacy is YET's most powerful and cost-effective recruitment engine ‚Äî and it is currently entirely unstructured, meaning significant untapped potential remains.

üî•Test of Fire: The Defining Transformative Moment

Across all 5 cohorts, the Test of Fire ‚Äî live facilitation to real youth audiences ‚Äî was the single most cited transformational experience. Its impact is undisputed; however, its logistical support and preparation time require meaningful improvement in future iterations.

üöåTransport: The Primary Equity Barrier

26 of 48 alumni cited daily commuting costs as their biggest personal challenge. The non-residential format creates a structural socioeconomic barrier that affects participation quality disproportionately for alumni from peri-urban and rural backgrounds.

üìãInformation Gaps Create Misaligned Expectations

While 73% found recruitment information very clear, critical gaps remain around retention criteria, post-YET career pathways, and logistical requirements ‚Äî generating anxiety and misaligned expectations that colour the training experience before it even begins.

üè°Residential Format: The #1 Structural Request

Across every cohort surveyed, transitioning to a residential or hybrid-residential model was the most frequently and most passionately requested structural improvement ‚Äî seen not as a luxury, but as a prerequisite for equitable access to the full YET experience.

üí°

Stakeholder Bottom Line

YET is delivering measurable, transformational outcomes ‚Äî particularly in facilitation confidence and professional network-building. The strategic priority for the next iteration is addressing structural barriers: transport costs, residential access, and pre-training information quality. These are solvable problems with significant return on investment.

Recruitment & Information Clarity

3 insight themes identified ¬∑ Synthesised from open-text responses ¬∑ All quotes anonymised

‚ùì Retention & Employment Ambiguity 18 mentions

Many alumni entered YET without understanding that retention at Educate! is merit-based, competitive, and not guaranteed. This created anxiety during training and frustration post-programme, as expectations were unmet.

Alumni repeatedly described hearing about retention opportunities only through rumour or informal channels. The absence of transparent, upfront communication about criteria, numbers retained, and the decision timeline meant many trained with an implicit assumption of employment that was never formally offered.
"Making it clear that the job doesn't come automatically ‚Äî it takes time and is on merit."
"I only heard through rumors that the best youth are retained as trainers. It wasn't clearly communicated."
"The numbers who will be retained and how a participant knows they are doing well should be shared."
üí°

Implication: Standardise a pre-training information document that explicitly addresses: (1) retention criteria, (2) the proportion typically retained per cohort, (3) timeline for retention decisions, and (4) what success during YET looks like in assessable terms.

üó∫Ô∏è Post-YET Pathways Insufficiently Articulated 12 mentions

Alumni wanted clearer articulation of the career pathways, certifications, and transferable skills gained ‚Äî especially how YET connects to future employment, further study, or entrepreneurship opportunities.

Recruitment messaging focused primarily on values and mission alignment rather than the concrete, individual benefits of participation. Alumni who enrolled expecting immediate startup capital or business skills felt the programme's emphasis on mindset and facilitation was misaligned with what was communicated at recruitment.
"The main goal of Educate! was not well explained."
"The program emphasizes mindset over immediate startup tools, which felt mismatched during recruitment."
"Post-training obligations and opportunities needed more clarity from the beginning."
üí°

Implication: Create a YET Graduate Value Proposition one-pager covering: skills earned, facilitation certification, and career options alumni can pursue ‚Äî employment, freelance facilitation, alumni networks, further training pathways.

üìã Logistical Details Not Communicated in Advance 9 mentions

Key logistical facts ‚Äî accommodation arrangements, venue location, programme intensity, transport requirements, and the non-residential nature of training ‚Äî were surprises for multiple alumni that affected their planning and performance.

Several alumni arrived unprepared for the physical and financial demands of daily commuting. Others were caught off guard by requirements like the UGX 50,000 contribution, the need to arrange their own accommodation, or the expectation to mobilise youth independently for the Test of Fire.
"Accommodation preparations for proper planning ‚Äî we needed to know earlier."
"Information around the 50k requirements and the intensity of it all would have been helpful upfront."
"The training period, certificate issuance, and non-residential nature were not communicated in advance."
üí°

Implication: Issue a detailed Logistics Brief at least 3 weeks before training commences, covering: venue location and transport routes, daily schedule and intensity expectations, any financial contributions required, accommodation options, and Test of Fire preparation requirements.

Interview Experience

3 insight themes identified ¬∑ Synthesised from open-text responses ¬∑ All quotes anonymised

‚≠ê Practical & Interactive Format: Highly Valued 28 mentions

Alumni strongly praised the hands-on, conversational nature of the interview process. Mock selling exercises, idea pitching, and one-on-one panel formats made the experience feel human, relevant, and energising rather than intimidating.

Unlike traditional interviews, the YET process placed candidates in real facilitation and entrepreneurship scenarios ‚Äî asking them to sell products to panelists, pitch business ideas, and demonstrate communication skills in live settings. This format was described as a highlight, reflecting the spirit of YET itself.
"I just loved how interactive the whole session was ‚Äî so relieving."
"I was tasked to sell a product to the panelists ‚Äî I loved it."
"Being asked if I had a business idea was eye-opening."
"The one-on-one format with a recruitment team member was the best part."
üí°

Implication: Formally document and protect the practical, experiential interview format as a YET standard operating procedure to prevent drift toward conventional interview approaches across cohorts and locations.

üìç Venue Accessibility Creates Participation Barriers 14 mentions

Interview venues were too far from many candidates, requiring costly, tiring, and sometimes multi-hour travel ‚Äî creating a socioeconomic barrier that may have excluded candidates from peri-urban and rural areas before they even entered the room.

Multiple alumni described travelling from districts such as Bugiri, Iganga, and surrounding areas to reach interview venues in Jinja. The process of obtaining supporting documents such as LC letters also added administrative burden disproportionately affecting those furthest from urban centres.
"Interview venues should be in areas central to most people."
"Getting the LC letter was a hustle ‚Äî the national ID is a clear indication of residence."
"Virtual interviews for candidates who are far should be considered."
üí°

Implication: (1) Pilot hybrid virtual interviews for candidates located more than 40km from the nearest venue. (2) Prioritise centrally located physical venues accessible by public transport. (3) Review documentation requirements for accessibility.

‚è±Ô∏è Interview-to-Training Timeline Too Compressed 10 mentions

The window between receiving a selection notification and commencing training was far too short ‚Äî sometimes as little as one week ‚Äî leaving candidates insufficient time to arrange finances, inform employers or families, and prepare mentally and logistically.

This compression was especially difficult for candidates who needed to relocate, arrange childcare, request leave from employment, or save transport funds. Several described arriving at training stressed and under-resourced as a direct result of the tight timeline.
"Timelines were too fixed. We had interviews this week and next week were required to relocate."
"After the decision I needed more time to prepare financially."
"The time between selection and training start was too tight ‚Äî it affected how ready I felt."
üí°

Implication: Establish a minimum mandatory gap of 14 days between interview decision notification and training commencement. Build this into the programme calendar as a non-negotiable operational standard.

Post-Interview Communication

2 insight themes identified ¬∑ Synthesised from open-text responses ¬∑ All quotes anonymised

‚úÖ Multi-Channel Communication: A Proven Strength 25 mentions

Alumni widely praised the post-selection communication approach ‚Äî combining email confirmations, direct phone calls from Programme Officers, and WhatsApp group onboarding. This multi-channel model reduced anxiety, built excitement, and ensured information reached everyone.

The combination of a formal email (providing documented reference), a personal phone call (building relationship and allowing Q&A), and a WhatsApp group (facilitating peer connection before training began) was described as thorough, warm, and professional. Several alumni noted this early communication set a positive tone for the entire training experience.
"I received feedback in time ‚Äî they used both emails and calling, plus follow-ups."
"An email was sent accompanied by a phone call. All questions were answered."
"A direct call from one of the Program Officers, plus a follow-up email."
"I was sent an email to join a WhatsApp group where everything was communicated."
üí°

Implication: (1) Formally document the multi-channel communication sequence as an SOP: email ‚Üí phone call within 48hrs ‚Üí WhatsApp group onboarding. (2) Train all recruitment staff on this sequence. (3) Establish a standard response time commitment ‚Äî all queries responded to within 24 hours.

ü§ù Unsuccessful Candidates Deserve Greater Dignity 5 mentions

Candidates who were not selected reported receiving insufficient feedback, unclear notification timelines, and in some cases no formal communication at all. This represents both a missed opportunity and a reputational risk for YET and Educate!

While the volume of applicants makes individualised feedback challenging, the complete absence of structured rejection communication was described as demoralising and inconsistent with YET's stated values of dignity and empowerment. Some alumni only learned they were unsuccessful through informal channels.
"To those that weren't considered, better can be done to align expectations."
"The timelines made me feel uncertain about the process."
"I needed more clarity on outcomes ‚Äî I heard nothing official."
üí°

Implication: (1) Develop a compassionate, standardised rejection template that acknowledges effort, provides brief constructive reasoning, and encourages reapplication. (2) Commit to notifying all unsuccessful candidates within 5 business days of selection decisions being finalised.

Training Content & Delivery

5 insight themes identified ¬∑ Synthesised from open-text responses ¬∑ All quotes anonymised

üî•Test of Fire: The Signature Capstone Experience29 mentions

The Test of Fire ‚Äî where YETs independently design and facilitate a real session to live youth audiences ‚Äî was simultaneously the most celebrated and most logistically challenged element. Alumni described it as transformational but under-resourced.

Alumni universally agreed that the Test of Fire represented the zenith of their YET experience ‚Äî the moment theory became practice and confidence became real. However, preparation time was widely reported as insufficient, and the expectation to independently mobilise youth participants without transport support placed an unfair burden on trainees.
"Test of Fire gave me confidence to design and facilitate to real participants. Irreplaceable."
"The test of fire was amazing ‚Äî I applied event management skills during E!BA bootcamp 2025."
"Mobilizing youths without transport facilitation was a hard nut to crack."
"Test of fire preparation time could be increased ‚Äî we needed at least 2 more days."
üí°

Implication: (1) Retain Test of Fire as YET's non-negotiable signature capstone. (2) Increase dedicated preparation time by a minimum of 2 days. (3) Introduce transport reimbursement for participant mobilisation. (4) Consider adding a second, smaller practical session earlier in the programme.

üéìFacilitation & Knowledge Shares: Universally Valued38 mentions

Knowledge shares, peer mock facilitations, and the structured facilitation rubric were the most consistently valued elements across all cohorts. Alumni credited these with fundamentally transforming their professional communication, teaching ability, and self-confidence.

The facilitation rubric ‚Äî providing clear, observable criteria for what excellent facilitation looks like ‚Äî was cited as particularly powerful, giving alumni a shared professional language and a tool they continued to use in subsequent work. Mock facilitations provided a safe space to fail, iterate, and grow before facing real audiences.
"The art of facilitation has built me professionally. I am more confident now than ever."
"The facilitation rubric and distinction between knowledge-based and practice-based lessons were very helpful."
"Mocks are what I remember most ‚Äî they brought out my facilitation and collaboration skills."
üí°

Implication: (1) Protect knowledge shares and mocks as non-negotiable curriculum elements ‚Äî never cut under scheduling pressure. (2) Formally publish and distribute the facilitation rubric as a YET Graduate Tool alumni can use in their ongoing work.

üõ°Ô∏èSafeguarding: High-Stakes Content, Insufficient Time8 mentions

Safeguarding and child protection content was recognised as essential and important, but delivered under significant time pressure ‚Äî reducing comprehension of one of the most legally and ethically critical components of the programme.

YETs work directly with young people in schools and communities. Adequate understanding of safeguarding principles, mandatory reporting obligations, and child protection protocols is a professional and legal requirement. The current delivery model does not adequately reflect this importance in its allocation of time.
"The safeguarding part was unclear ‚Äî content was okay but we were a bit rushed."
"Safeguarding and child protection requires enough time ‚Äî it's too important to rush."
"I needed more information on child protection ‚Äî there was really a lot of content to absorb."
üí°

Implication: (1) Dedicate a protected extended session ‚Äî minimum half-day, ideally a full day ‚Äî that is explicitly non-compressible. (2) Introduce pre-reading materials sent before training. (3) Consider a post-training online assessment to verify understanding before alumni work with young people.

üè†Non-Residential Format: The Biggest Structural Gap22 mentions

Daily commuting from home was the single most disruptive structural factor ‚Äî draining participants financially and physically, reducing after-hours preparation quality, limiting peer bonding, and cutting off access to late-evening trainer support.

The impact creates a two-tier training experience where alumni from closer proximity had meaningfully better access to preparation time, peer collaboration, and trainer support than those commuting long distances. This is a fundamental equity issue that deserves strategic attention at the programme design level.
"Commuting from Iganga to Jinja meant I'd arrive home too late to call trainers for support."
"I was coming from Bugiri to Jinja every day ‚Äî financially draining. But I acquired so much knowledge."
"Traveling long distances every day required me to fund myself ‚Äî no one knew this was coming."
üí°

Implication: (1) Commission a feasibility and cost analysis for a residential or hybrid-residential model. (2) As an interim measure, introduce a daily transport stipend for participants travelling more than 30km. (3) Explore partnerships with local accommodation providers near training venues.

üîÅContent Repetition & Unclear Activity Design8 mentions

A vocal minority cited repetitive knowledge share topics and poorly explained game-based activities as weaknesses that reduced engagement and created inefficiency during what is already an intensive training period.

The GROW feedback model and the Goal Setting Bingo activity were specifically cited as poorly introduced. Knowledge share repetition was noted particularly by alumni who had participated in related Educate! programmes before YET, suggesting a need for differentiated content pathways.
"Repetitive knowledge shares ‚Äî some content felt like we'd covered it before in a previous session."
"The goal setting Bingo game ‚Äî instructions were not clear and it confused me for most of the activity."
"The GROW model of feedback was not explained clearly enough before we were asked to use it."
üí°

Implication: (1) Conduct a curriculum audit to identify and consolidate redundant knowledge share topics. (2) Pilot-test all game-based activities with a small group before full delivery. (3) Introduce a facilitator checklist for activities requiring prior knowledge.

Overall Experience & Key Outcomes

5 insight themes identified ¬∑ Synthesised from open-text responses ¬∑ All quotes anonymised

üöÄFacilitation Mastery: YET's Defining Transformative Outcome38 mentions

The overwhelming majority of alumni ‚Äî across all cohorts, locations, and backgrounds ‚Äî identified facilitation skills and public speaking confidence as the single most life-changing outcome of YET. This is the programme's core value proposition and should lead all stakeholder communications.

Alumni described measurable, observable changes in their professional lives: leading school assemblies, facilitating community workshops, presenting to employers, and delivering training to peers and colleagues. The confidence built through repeated practice in a structured, supportive environment was described as durable and career-defining.
"The facilitation skill I got from YET is second to none ‚Äî I use it in every professional context."
"YET changed my entrepreneurial mindset and gave me confidence in mass communication."
"I became a very vibrant public speaker ‚Äî something I never thought I could be."
üí°

Implication: (1) Formally position facilitation mastery and public speaking confidence as YET's primary advertised outcomes in all recruitment, stakeholder reporting, and external communications. (2) Document and publish alumni facilitation success stories. (3) Explore facilitation certification pathways that formally recognise this skill.

üöåTransport & Financial Burden: A Persistent Equity Barrier26 mentions

Daily transport cost was the most consistently cited personal hardship across all cohorts ‚Äî not merely an inconvenience, but a fundamental equity and access barrier that shaped how fully participants could engage with training, preparation, and peer collaboration.

The financial strain was particularly acute for alumni from districts more than 30km from training venues. Several described arriving late, leaving early, or missing optional evening sessions due to transport constraints. The cumulative burden ‚Äî potentially UGX 10,000‚Äì20,000 daily over a multi-week period ‚Äî represented a significant portion of available resources.
"Commuting from home became expensive. Getting daily transport was a persistent challenge."
"I was coming from Bugiri to Jinja every day ‚Äî financially draining, but I pressed on."
"Some days I would wake up not knowing where I'd find transport money."
üí°

Implication: (1) Introduce a tiered daily transport allowance: full allowance for participants travelling more than 50km, partial for 30‚Äì50km. (2) Explore bulk transport arrangements to reduce per-participant cost. (3) Build transport support as a line item in future programme budgets.

üè°Residential Training: The Single Most Requested Change19 mentions

Across all cohorts, transitioning to a residential or hybrid-residential model was the most frequently and most passionately requested structural improvement ‚Äî seen not as a luxury but as a prerequisite for equitable access to the full YET experience.

Alumni described a residential model as a prerequisite for equitable access. Those with informal accommodation near training venues consistently reported a richer, more immersive experience. The current model inadvertently creates two different experiences of the same programme based on geography and financial resources.
"If YET were residential, participants would have ample time to prepare and get late-night support from trainers."
"Make it both residential and non-residential so participants can choose based on their situation."
"Even if only for some sites ‚Äî residential YET would transform the experience for everyone."
üí°

Implication: (1) Commission a detailed feasibility study and cost-benefit analysis for a residential model for at least one cohort per year. (2) Identify accommodation partnerships near existing training venues. (3) As a near-term measure, consider a hybrid model where participants opt in to subsidised shared accommodation.

üåêProfessional Networks: A Durable, Underrated Outcome14 mentions

The professional and personal networks formed through YET were described as enduring, career-enhancing, and deeply valued ‚Äî yet they largely formed organically without institutional support, structure, or alumni engagement infrastructure.

YET alumni reported continuing to collaborate with, refer work to, and draw professional support from fellow graduates years after completing training. This organic alumni network represents a significant institutional asset that is currently under-cultivated and under-leveraged by Educate!
"A very big circle of friends and family that have built me into the person I am now."
"Connecting me to like-minded individuals was one of the best outcomes I didn't expect."
"The network I built ‚Äî I carry it with me everywhere. I still work with people I met in YET."
üí°

Implication: (1) Create a structured YET Alumni Engagement Platform ‚Äî a digital community of practice with regular touchpoints, mentorship matching, and shared resources. (2) Hold an annual YET Alumni Convening. (3) Establish an alumni directory accessible to all YET graduates.

üí¨What Alumni Want Next: Three Priority Additions30 mentions

The most frequently requested additions centred on three clear priorities: more practical facilitation experiences, structured post-training mentorship, and introductory financial literacy content ‚Äî alongside strong interest in online or modular delivery options.

Demand for additional practicals reflected alumni's recognition that the Test of Fire's impact would compound with repetition. Mentorship requests reflected the desire for continuity of learning beyond the formal training period. Financial literacy was seen as the missing link between facilitation skill and sustainable livelihood impact.
"Provision for online modular sessions with certification for those who can't attend physically."
"Peer Coaching and Mentorship Sessions: participants learning from each other post-YET."
"Participants need at least 2 practicals ‚Äî not just one test of fire ‚Äî to build real confidence."
"A youth livelihood programme: introductions to advanced financial literacy and enterprise management."
üí°

Implication: Three immediate strategic additions: (1) Post-training peer mentorship programme pairing current YETs with experienced alumni for 3‚Äì6 months. (2) A second, structured practical facilitation session mid-programme. (3) A 3-hour introductory financial literacy module covering budgeting, savings, and basic enterprise management.
